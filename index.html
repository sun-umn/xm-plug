Project overview

Unifying plug-in methods for inverse problem solving

This project organizes our group's plug-in inverse problem solvers under one shared language: solve inverse problems by optimizing through a pretrained generative prior, while keeping the measurement model explicit.

Master equation

Typical MAP formulation

Traditional ideas for solving IPs rely on optimization, often motivated by the Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) principle:

\[ \widehat{x} = \arg\min_x \ell\!\left(y, \mathcal{A}(x)\right) + \lambda \Omega(x) \]

Here \(y\) is the measurement, \(\mathcal{A}\) is the forward operator, \(\ell(y,\mathcal{A}(x))\) enforces measurement fidelity, and \(\Omega(x)\) is a handcrafted or learned prior regularizer.

Interleaving vs. plug-in methods

The key distinction is whether measurement correction is interleaved with generation, or whether measurement consistency is optimized through a fixed pretrained prior. This comparison motivates the first driving question: how can diffusion priors solve inverse problems while keeping the reconstruction natural, measurement-consistent, and robust to noise?

Question 1 · DMPlug

How to improve the measurement and manifold feasibility with robustness to noise?

DMPlug answers this question by considering the diffusion generation process as a function. Instead of alternating reverse diffusion with correction steps, it optimizes a <\measurement-aware> optimization objective through the pretrained diffusion prior:

DMPlug formulation

\[ \widehat{z} = \arg\min_z \ell\!\left(y, \mathcal{A}(G_\theta(z))\right) + \lambda R(z), \qquad \widehat{x}=G_\theta(\widehat{z}) \]

The pretrained model \(G_\theta\) is kept fixed and plugged into the inverse problem as the prior. Optimization happens over the latent variable \(z\), while the measurement loss remains explicit.

The diffusion model keeps the solution close to the learned natural-object manifold, while the explicit loss enforces the forward measurement model.

From broad generation to specific reconstruction

The issue is not that foundation flow-matching models are weak generative models. Rather, they are broad generators, so by themselves they may not impose sufficiently specific constraints for reconstructing one measured object.

This motivates the second driving question: how can inverse problems make better use of foundation diffusion priors?

We propose an instance-guided warm-start strategy to address this issue: FMPlug.

Instance-guided warm-start

\[ \min_{z,\,t\in[0,1]} \ell\!\left( y,\, \mathcal{A}\circ G_\theta(\alpha_t y+\beta_t z, t) \right) \quad \mathrm{s.t.}\quad z\in S^{d-1}_{\epsilon}(0,\sqrt{d}) \]

FMPlug uses the relation between the unknown object \(x\) and the measurement \(y\): when they are close, we do not need to generate from scratch at \(t=0\). Instead, we plug \(y\) into an appropriate learnable time point on the flow path, creating a shortcut for reconstruction. The shell constraint keeps the latent seed Gaussian-like, preserving the source structure expected by the foundation FM model.

FMPlug expands plug-in methods to scientific inverse problems

Scientific inverse problems often have sparse paired data and a strong domain shift between measurements and objects: the measurement may be Fourier, scattered-field, or nonlinear sensor data, while the object is the scientific image we want to recover. In this regime, the measurement itself may not be a good warm-start guide. FMPlug uses a few object-domain instances instead, turning scarce scientific examples into an instance prior for the foundation FM model.

Few-shot scientific objective

\[ \min_{z,\,t,\,w} \ell\!\left( y,\, \mathcal{A}\circ G_\theta \left(\alpha_t\sum_{k=1}^{K}w_k x_k+\beta_t z,t\right) \right) \quad \mathrm{s.t.}\quad z\in S^{d-1}_{\epsilon}(0,\sqrt{d}),\, t\in[0,1],\,w\in\Delta_K \]

The simplex weights \(w\) select and combine the most relevant few-shot instances \(\{x_k\}_{k=1}^{K}\). This replaces direct measurement warm-starting with object-domain guidance, which is better aligned with scientific reconstruction under measurement-object domain shift.
